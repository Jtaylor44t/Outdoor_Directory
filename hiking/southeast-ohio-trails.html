Best Hiking Trails in Southeast Ohio

Experience the dramatic beauty of Hocking Hills with towering waterfalls, deep gorges, massive caves, and stunning rock formations

Explore Southeast Ohio's Magnificent Hocking Hills Region

Southeast Ohio is home to the spectacular Hocking Hills State Park, one of Ohio's most treasured natural wonders. This ancient landscape features deep hemlock gorges, towering 150-foot cliffs carved by retreating glaciers, cascading waterfalls, massive recess caves, and unique rock formations created over millions of years. The region offers some of the most dramatic and photogenic hiking trails in the entire Midwest.

Old Man's Cave Trail

Hocking Hills State Park
1.0 Miles
45 Minutes
150 Elevation

The crown jewel of Hocking Hills, this trail winds through a spectacular gorge featuring the Upper, Middle, and Lower Falls, Devil's Bathtub, and the legendary Old Man's Cave - a massive recess cave named after a hermit who lived there in the 1800s. Stunning rock formations and hemlock-shaded paths make this an unforgettable hike.

Ash Cave Trail

Hocking Hills State Park
0.25 Miles
15 Minutes
Minimal Elevation

Ohio's largest recess cave is an awe-inspiring horseshoe-shaped amphitheater spanning 700 feet wide and 100 feet deep. An accessible paved path leads to this magnificent natural cathedral where a waterfall cascades 90 feet over the lip during wet seasons. Perfect for all ages and abilities.

Cedar Falls Trail

Hocking Hills State Park
1.0 Miles

Follow Queer Creek through a pristine hemlock gorge to reach the beautiful Cedar Falls, which plunges 50 feet into a scenic pool surrounded by black hand sandstone cliffs. The trail features wooden stairs and bridges, offering stunning views of the waterfall and unique rock formations.

Hocking Hills State Park
2.5 Miles
1.5-2 Hours

Hocking Hills' deepest gorge features 200-foot towering cliffs - the highest in Ohio. Choose the easy gorge floor trail through pristine old-growth forest, or challenge yourself on the rim trail offering breathtaking cliff-top views. This natural area showcases rare plants and stunning geological formations.

Rock House Trail

Hocking Hills State Park
1.0 Miles
45 Minutes
200 Elevation

Hocking Hills' only true cave is a corridor-like passage carved into a massive cliff face, featuring natural "windows" looking out over the valley below. This unique geological formation served as a shelter for Native Americans and pioneers. The trail includes stairs and offers spectacular views through the cave's seven window-like openings.

Essential Hiking Tips for Southeast Ohio

Trail Conditions

Hocking Hills trails can be slippery, especially near waterfalls and caves. Wear proper footwear with good traction. Many trails feature stairs and uneven terrain. Check the Ohio DNR website for current trail conditions and any closures before your visit.

Best Times to Visit

Spring offers peak waterfall flow from snowmelt and rain. Fall features spectacular foliage (mid-October). Winter creates magical ice formations at waterfalls and caves. Weekdays are less crowded than weekends, especially during peak seasons.

Photography Tips

Hocking Hills is incredibly photogenic year-round. Early morning and late afternoon offer the best lighting. Bring a tripod for waterfall shots. The caves and gorges create unique lighting conditions perfect for dramatic photos.

Parking & Access

Parking lots fill quickly on weekends and holidays, especially at Old Man's Cave. Arrive early (before 10 AM) or visit on weekdays. Some areas require parking passes. Multiple trails have separate access points - plan your route accordingly.

Safety Reminders

Stay on marked trails - cliff edges can be dangerous. Don't climb on rocks or cliffs. Trails can be icy in winter. Keep children close, especially near cliff edges and waterfalls. Cell service is limited in some areas.

Leave No Trace

Pack out all trash. Stay on designated trails to protect fragile ecosystems and rare plants. Don't remove rocks, plants, or artifacts. Respect wildlife and other visitors. These ancient formations took millions of years to form - help preserve them.
